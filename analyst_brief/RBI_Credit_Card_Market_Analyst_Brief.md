# India Credit Card Market — Analyst Brief
### FY2022-23 to FY2025-26 | Source: RBI Bank-wise ATM/POS/Card Statistics

**Scope:** 49 monthly filings (March 2022 – April 2026), 66 issuers across Public Sector, Private Sector, Foreign, Payment Bank, and Small Finance Bank categories. All annual figures follow the Indian fiscal year convention (April–March), consistent with RBI reporting standards.

---

## Executive Summary

India's credit card market grew from an estimated 73.6 million cards (March 2022) to 118.63 million (March 2026) — a 61% expansion — but the growth profile shifted sharply along the way. Issuance accelerated through FY23-24 (+19.3% YoY) before decelerating to a stable ~7.6–8% YoY pace by FY24-25 and FY25-26, marking a clear transition from an expansion phase into market maturity. Spend value grew faster than card count every year (Rs 14.32 → Rs 23.62 lakh crore), driven not by bigger-ticket purchases but by **more frequent, smaller-value transactions** — the average transaction size fell from roughly Rs 5,200 to Rs 3,970 industry-wide. Competitively, the top six issuers (HDFC, SBI, ICICI, Axis, Kotak, RBL) have held their ranks for four straight years, but two regulatory events — a 2022 dormant-card closure mandate and a 2024 RBI restriction on Kotak — materially reshaped individual issuer trajectories. Risk indicators improved structurally (industry ATM-cash usage nearly halved, 29.2% → 18.5%), with risk now concentrated in a small number of identifiable, persistent outliers rather than spread system-wide.

## 1. Market Overview: From Expansion to Maturity

| Metric | FY23 | FY24 | FY25 | FY26 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cards Outstanding | 85.30M | 101.8M | 109.88M | 118.63M |
| YoY Growth | 15.86% | **19.34% (peak)** | 7.94% | 7.6% |
| Total Annual Spend | Rs 14.32L cr | Rs 18.31L cr | Rs 21.09L cr | Rs 23.62L cr |

Growth did not decelerate continuously — it **peaked in FY23-24**, then underwent a structural slowdown. FY23 and FY24 card counts and spend values were independently cross-checked against published RBI/industry figures and matched within 1%, validating the dataset's accuracy at the headline level.

A sharp, isolated dip in card count occurred in August–September 2022 (-2.82%, -0.37% MoM). This is fully explained by RBI's Master Direction on Credit & Debit Cards (effective July 1, 2022), which mandated closure of cards unused for over a year — a one-time regulatory cleanup, not a demand-side weakness.

**Key reframe:** Spend value is growing faster than card count, but **not because of premiumization**. Average transaction size has fallen every year, confirmed independently through two separate calculation methods across two different dashboard views. Credit cards are increasingly used for frequent, everyday, lower-value spend rather than large discretionary purchases.

## 2. Competitive Landscape: A Stable Oligopoly, Shifting at the Edges

**Top 6 issuers, unchanged in rank for 4 consecutive years:** HDFC > SBI > ICICI > Axis > Kotak > RBL

| Issuer | FY23 Share | FY26 Share | Driver |
|---|---|---|---|
| HDFC | 20.56% (post-dip; 22.4% pre-dip) | 22.18% | Recovered from 2022 regulatory shock — not new growth |
| Kotak | 5.8% | 3.9% | RBI barred new card issuance Apr 2024–Feb 2025; card base shrank ~17% during the ban |
| IDFC First | 1.8% | 3.83% | Steady organic growth (~0.6–0.7 pts/year); now within 0.07 pts of overtaking Kotak |

**Two regulatory events explain the largest single-issuer movements, both independently confirmed against external sources:**
- **Axis Bank's 2.3M card addition spike (FY22-23)** was the Citi India consumer banking migration, completed March 1, 2023 — not organic acquisition. Confirmed by Axis's unusually low spend-per-card that year (Rs 13.6k), consistent with a bulk transfer of not-yet-engaged accounts.
- **Kotak's share collapse** is a direct, dated consequence of the RBI restriction — its card base fell from 6M+ to 5.02M during the ban itself.

**Premium spend leadership:** American Express leads decisively and is widening its lead — spend-per-card rose every year, Rs 29.4k → Rs 50.2k (+71% over 4 years), with no reversals. Karur Vysya Bank is the only other issuer present in the premium tier across all four years — a smaller but structurally consistent niche player.

**Two figures excluded from analysis after verification:** Equitas SFB's apparent #1 spend-per-card ranking (FY24-25) was based on a card base of just 324 — statistically meaningless. Bank of America's similar spike (FY23-24) was based on 4 transactions nationwide. Both flagged, not featured.

**CSB Bank** appeared to "decline" in spend-per-card (Rs 32.2k → Rs 15.92k), but its card base grew 30x (6,460 → 195,485) over the same period — this is a scale-up growth story with natural dilution, not a customer-quality decline.

## 3. Customer Value: Premiumization Is Narrow, Not Broad

**HDFC is the standout performer** — the only major issuer growing card base *and* spend-per-card simultaneously (card base +50%, spend/card Rs 21.46k → Rs 24.83k over the period), while every peer trades off one for the other.

**IndusInd Bank's premium decline is confirmed across three independent metrics** — falling spend-per-card, a shift from above- to below-industry-average transaction value by FY25-26, and persistent negative value/volume divergence (-0.10, FY24-25). This consistency across unrelated calculations makes it one of the most defensible individual findings in the analysis.

**Online adoption is led by Foreign banks.** "Small Finance Bank" category figures are driven almost entirely by AU Small Finance Bank — the rest of the category has negligible card activity and should not be read as a sector-wide trend.

**A notable reversal of the industry's online-first norm:** by FY25-26, IDFC First (57% PoS) and Yes Bank (55–58% PoS) run majority-offline books — a deliberate divergence from the broader market, not yet explained by available data.

## 4. Risk Profile: Structural, Not Systemic

- **Industry ATM-cash usage (credit cards):** 29.16% → 27.03% → 20.31% → 18.54% — a sustained, ~36% relative decline, signaling healthier overall product usage.
- **Canara Bank is the persistent #1 risk outlier in every single year** (9.2% → 7.9% → 6.9% → 5.9%) — improving in line with the industry, but never relinquishing the top spot. This is a structural pattern tied to its customer base, not a temporary anomaly, and the clearest standing risk finding in the dataset.
- **Public Sector banks carry consistently higher cash-reliance than Private or Foreign banks**, once the noisy Small Finance category (an AU SFB artifact) is set aside.
- **Bandhan Bank's apparent 15.3% risk spike (FY23-24)** is a market-entry artifact — its credit card book launched in March 2024 with a near-zero base (~Rs 1.23 lakh total monthly spend) — not an emerging risk trend. It reverted to the lowest risk tier by FY25-26.
- **DCB Bank and ESAF SFB's flagged divergence scores (FY24-25)** stem from thin transaction bases (480 and 4,414 monthly transactions nationwide, respectively) — statistical noise, not genuine anomalies.

---

## Strategic Recommendations

1. **Treat Canara Bank's elevated cash-withdrawal ratio as a structural, segment-level risk** — not transient — and prioritize it for targeted portfolio monitoring rather than industry-wide blanket controls.
2. **The premium card segment is winnable without mass scale.** AmEx and Karur Vysya sustain premium positioning with focused, smaller portfolios — a viable strategy for any issuer prioritizing yield over volume.
3. **Reframe product strategy around frequency, not ticket size.** Falling average transaction values industry-wide suggest the next growth phase favors high-frequency, low-ticket use cases (daily retail, food delivery, micro-merchant categories) over premium big-ticket positioning.
4. **Regulatory compliance has a quantifiable, multi-year market-share cost.** Kotak's ~2 percentage-point share loss tied directly to a 10-month IT-compliance restriction is a concrete, citable case study for any risk or compliance function building a business case for proactive IT governance investment.

## Methodology Notes & Data Caveats

- **Source:** RBI Bank-wise ATM/POS/Card Statistics, 49 monthly raw filings (March 2022 – April 2026), standardized through a Python-based ETL pipeline and validated in PostgreSQL.
- **Scope decision:** Analysis begins March 2022, following a structural change in RBI's reporting methodology that year; the COVID-affected period (2020–2021) is excluded to maintain metric comparability across the full dataset.
- **External validation:** FY23 and FY24 aggregate card counts and spend values were cross-checked against independently published RBI/industry figures and matched within 1%.
- **Exclusions:** Equitas SFB, Bank of America, and Bandhan Bank (FY23-24) figures were excluded from headline claims due to statistically immaterial transaction or card bases in the relevant period.
- **Category caveat:** "Small Finance Bank" category averages are materially driven by AU Small Finance Bank alone; the remaining issuers in this category have negligible credit card activity throughout the analysis period.
- **Fiscal year convention:** All annual figures use the Indian fiscal year (April–March), consistent with RBI and Indian banking sector reporting norms.
